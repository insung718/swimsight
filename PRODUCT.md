# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Competitive swimmers ages 10-18, swim coaches, school swim teams, club swimmers, and friends or teammates who want private comparison without public pressure. Athletes use SwimSight after meets and training sessions to understand progress, goals, and what to focus on next. Coaches use it to review team development, compare swimmers responsibly, and guide training conversations.

## Product Purpose

SwimSight turns raw swim times, goals, meets, training, and community context into clear performance analytics. Success means a swimmer can enter a result and immediately understand personal bests, trends, prediction confidence, data quality, event strengths, and the next practical action. The product should feel official enough for real teams while still motivating enough for young athletes.

## Brand Personality

Premium, focused, athletic.

SwimSight should feel like Apple-grade sports analytics for swimming: clean, fast, calm, precise, and visually memorable. It should avoid looking like a generic AI SaaS template, a cluttered spreadsheet, or a social leaderboard that makes athletes feel worse.

## Anti-references

- Generic AI dashboards with too much explanatory copy, repeated glass cards, and purple-blue gradients.
- Public ego-war leaderboards that discourage swimmers.
- Overly cinematic experiments that create lag, unreadable text, or mobile layout problems.
- Demo-data-heavy products where signed-in users cannot tell what is actually theirs.
- Coach tools that expose swimmer data without explicit membership.

## Design Principles

1. Data earns trust before it makes claims.
2. Self-improvement is the default comparison.
3. The dashboard should reduce cognitive load, not explain every calculation at once.
4. Motion should make the product feel alive while staying fast on mobile.
5. Coach access must be intentional, scoped, and visibly private.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Aim for WCAG AA contrast wherever practical. Text must stay readable on glass surfaces, controls must be keyboard accessible, and reduced-motion users should not be forced through heavy animations. Translation support should make language switching persistent and predictable across public pages and authenticated dashboard surfaces.
